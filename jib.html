Camera jib

2020

In high school I regularly helped out recording and streaming live stage events such as dance or musical performances. I thought that there must be a better way to record than holding the camera in your hand or having a boring stationary stream from a tripod. I went looking around the school together with the rest of the camera crew and found an old 4 meter long steel pipe, as well as a massive tripod. We decided to turn this into a camera jib for getting much more interesting shots on stage.

The tilt of the camera is passively maintained by a long thin tube that runs parallel to the main arm. When the jib moves up or down, the parralellogram always keeps the camera upright. The yaw is actively controlled by a motor. The arm is balanced using some dumbbell weights hanging on the back.

The camera is attached to the jib via old internals of a moving head lamp. It contains a disc that be rotated using a stepper motor via a belt, perfect for yawing a camera. The stepper motor is connected to an Arduino via a stepper driver. An IMU (gyroscope + accelerometer) constantly measures the rotation of the jib and compensates the yaw via various profiles. It is also possible to manually control the yaw using a joystick on the handje of the jib. The Arduino can connect to a smartphone via Bluetooth. This way it is possible to change the way the yaw is compensated. By rotating the camera by a larger angle than the jib rotates, it is possible to orbit a specific location on stage. By rotating the camera by the same angle as the jib, the camera always points forward and it is possible to move in a semi-straight line. It is even possible to do a very primitive dolly zoom this way.

There is a screen at the user end of the jib that shows the live camera feed.
